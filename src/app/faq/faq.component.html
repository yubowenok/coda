How to Solve a Problem

Input and Output

Your solutions programs are always checked by a judge machine instead of a human. The machine runs your program with secret test data in addition to the sample inputs, and checks if your program's output matches exactly the correct answer. There are a few important things to keep in mind:
  • Your must always read from standard input (stdin in C++ and System.in in Java). You shall never read from a file, or pass the data as arguments to the main function.
  • You must always output to standard output (stdout in C++ and System.out in Java). You shall not output to a file.
  • Your output must be exactly the same as the correct answer, without any extra characters. You must remove any debugging printing or logs at stdout before submitting your program. stderr printing is allowed, but do not print excessively as I/O significantly slows down your program.

Judge Verdicts

Meanings of the verdicts are shown below:
  • Accepted: Your solution is correct. Hooray!
  • Wrong Answer: Your solution did not produce the expected output. You may have a bug in your code, or your algorithm is incorrect (e.g. not getting optimal answer).
  • Time Limit Exceeded: Your solution did not finish in the time limit. You may have an endless loop, or your algorithm is not efficient enought.
  • Run Time Error: Your solution aborted abnormally. There are many possible causes of run time error, such accessing array index out of bound, dividing by zero, using excessive memory, etc.
  • Compile Error: You code did not compile. Please refer to the compiler message on the submission page for more details.

Problemset Modes

Standard Problemset

The problemset has fixed starting time and ending time. You can submit after it starts and before it ends.

Selftest Problemset

The problemset has a fixed duration. Each participant has his/her own session of that duration to complete the problemset. You can start you session at any time between the problemset's starting time and ending time. But once you start your session, you cannot pause it, and must complete it within the duration. Only start the session when you have enough time to work on it.

For example, consider a selftest problemset that starts at Jan 23 3:00pm, ends at Jan 30 3:00pm, and has a duration of 3 hours. Suppose you start your session at Jan 25 3:00pm, then you must complete the problemset by Jan 25 6:00pm.

Your session cannot extend beyond the problemset's ending time. Do not start the session at the last minute. If you start your session at Jan 30 2:59pm, then you have to complete it in one minute.

Open Judge

Submissions are immediately queued to be judged during the problemset. Verdicts are released as soon as the submissions are processed.

Blind Judge

Submissions are judged after the problemset ends. Only the last submission to each subtask will count and be judged. You can submit as many times as you like, and there is no penalty for resubmitting.

Scoreboard shows your maximum possible score before the problemset ends, assuming all your final submissions are correct.

Be careful about resubmission. If you submit one solution that is correct, and then you submit another one that is wrong, the earlier correct submission will be ignored and you will not receive points.

Score Penalty

Each incorrect submission results in 10% loss of a subtask's score. You will get a minimum of 30% score if you solve the subtask in the end.

Time Penalty

Each incorrect submission results in 4 minutes extra time added to the problemset finish time.

Freebies

This is the number of incorrect submissions that are exempted from penalty. Each problem subtask has its own freebies.
